### **2024 Q7 a) Turing Test**

#### üìù High-Yield Exam Note
*   **Origin:** Proposed by **Alan Turing** in 1950 to answer the question, *"Can machines think?"*
*   **The Setup (Imitation Game):** 
    *   There are three participants: a **Human Interrogator**, a **Human Subject**, and a **Machine (AI)**. 
    *   They are in separate rooms and communicate only via text (to avoid bias from voice/appearance).
*   **The Process:** The interrogator asks questions to both the human and the machine.
*   **The Goal:** The machine tries to act like a human and fool the interrogator. 
*   **Pass Criteria:** If the interrogator **cannot reliably distinguish** between the human and the machine, the machine is said to have passed the test and possesses "Artificial Intelligence."
*   **Limitation:** It only tests behavior (how it acts), not actual consciousness or understanding (Searle‚Äôs Chinese Room Argument).

### **2024 Q7 b) Fuzzy Logic**

#### üìù High-Yield Exam Note
*   **Definition:** Fuzzy Logic is a form of many-valued logic where variables may have a **truth value ranging between 0 and 1**, instead of just "True" (1) or "False" (0).
*   **Concept:** It handles **partial truth** or "shades of gray." In the real world, things aren't just black or white (e.g., Temperature isn't just "Hot" or "Cold"; it can be "Warm," "Slightly Cool," etc.).
*   **Architecture Steps:**
    1.  **Fuzzification:** Converting "crisp" input (e.g., 35¬∞C) into fuzzy sets (e.g., "Very Hot").
    2.  **Inference Engine:** Applying "If-Then" fuzzy rules.
    3.  **Defuzzification:** Converting the fuzzy output back into a crisp action (e.g., set AC fan speed to 80%).
*   **Applications:** Automatic gearboxes, Vacuum cleaners, Air conditioners, and Washing machines.

### **2024 Q7 c) Reasoning under Uncertainty**

#### üìù High-Yield Exam Note (Short Note format)

**1. Definition:**
Reasoning under uncertainty is the process of making decisions or reaching conclusions when the available information is **incomplete, ambiguous, or noisy**. In such cases, the agent cannot be 100% certain about the state of the world.

**2. Sources of Uncertainty:**
*   **Incomplete Information:** Not having all the facts (e.g., a doctor not knowing a patient's full medical history).
*   **Noisy Data:** Sensors giving slightly incorrect readings (e.g., a GPS with a 5-meter error).
*   **Complexity:** The world is too complex to model every single possibility.

**3. How AI Handles Uncertainty (Methods):**
*   **Probability Theory:** The most common tool. It uses **Bayes' Theorem** to calculate the likelihood of an event based on prior knowledge.
    *   *Formula:* $P(A|B) = \frac{P(B|A) \cdot P(A)}{P(B)}$
*   **Fuzzy Logic:** Handling "degrees of truth" rather than just True/False.
*   **Certainty Factors:** Assigning a confidence score (e.g., 0.8) to a rule.

**4. Example (Medical Diagnosis):**
A patient has a cough. A cough *could* mean a common cold (90% chance) or lung cancer (1% chance). An AI must reason using these **probabilities** to suggest the most likely cause rather than giving a single "Yes/No" answer.
